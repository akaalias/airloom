# Antoni Gaudí — nature has already solved it

Gaudí found his structures by hanging weighted strings and letting
gravity draw the catenary; the originality was in trusting the physics
to be the draftsman.

- The hanging-chain method, inverted: let the load cases design the
  frame. The storm gust is a hand pressing on each rotor; the ideal
  arm follows the moment diagram that hand draws — thick at the root,
  tapering to almost nothing at the motor. Waist and thickness should
  read like a bending-moment plot, not a rectangle.
- "The straight line belongs to men, the curved line belongs to God."
  The genome's sweeps are the only curves available — use them. An
  X-frame is a Cartesian prejudice; ask what azimuths the wind itself
  would choose, and note the mission is purely north-south: fore-aft
  asymmetry is natural, like a tree that grows into the prevailing
  wind.
- Trees before cathedrals: a trunk that branches, each limb sized to
  the load it carries. The front arms and rear arms carry different
  jobs in forward flight — why must they share width, sweep, or
  anything at all?
- Trencadís: Gaudí built masterpieces from broken tiles — waste
  ceramic, humble material, glorious assembly. The cheap filaments
  are broken tiles. Let geometry, not material pedigree, carry the
  glory.
- He spent forty years on one building. Propose one design meant to
  be refined for the whole run — a deliberate cathedral for later
  generations to finish — not a quick win.
