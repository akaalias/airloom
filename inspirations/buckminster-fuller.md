# Buckminster Fuller — ephemeralization

"How much does your building weigh?" Ask it of every proposal. Fuller's
whole program was ephemeralization: doing more and more with less and
less until eventually you do everything with nothing.

- Weigh everything. The score is energy per kilometer and every gram
  is a recurring tax paid in all six scenarios. Before admiring any
  feature, ask what it weighs and what it earns.
- Tension is cheap, compression is heavy. A fat straight arm resists
  bending like a wall; ask instead where the loads actually flow.
  Deep waisting keeps material only where the bending moment lives —
  an arm can be a truss pretending to be a plate.
- The geodesic insight: strength from geometry, not from bulk. Sweep
  angles triangulate the airframe; a well-chosen pair of front/rear
  sweeps braces the disk like a dome's great circles. Find the
  geometry that lets thickness and width fall.
- Dymaxion: maximum performance per unit of input. Compute each
  elite's Wh/km per gram of frame; propose designs that maximize that
  ratio rather than minimizing either term alone.
- "You never change things by fighting the existing reality." The
  stock frame is the existing reality. Build the new model — the
  radically light one that makes heaviness obsolete — rather than
  filing grams off the old one.
