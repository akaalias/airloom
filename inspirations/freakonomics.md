# Freakonomics — incentives, loopholes, and hidden causes

Levitt and Dubner's move: assume the system responds to its actual
incentives, not its stated purpose, and go looking for the mispriced
margin everyone else ignores.

- The fitness function is the incentive scheme. Read it like a tax
  code: mean Wh/km + 0.5 × worst scenario. Sumo wrestlers throw
  matches exactly when the payoff table says to — where does this
  payoff table reward "throwing" a scenario? Maybe tanking an already
  bad scenario slightly to buy big mean improvements is underpriced.
- Question conventional wisdom: everyone "knows" carbon is best and
  stiff is good. The data-driven question is: what does the elite
  table actually show? If a cheap plastic is within noise of carbon,
  the consensus is a cargo cult — arbitrage it.
- Correlation is not causation: the elites share many gene values, but
  most are hitchhikers that rode along with one causal gene. Propose
  natural experiments — take an elite, change exactly one suspicious
  gene value dramatically, keep the rest — to find out which genes
  actually earn their keep.
- Look for the mispriced margin: every design so far has probably
  spent effort on drag and mass. What is the cheap, unglamorous gene
  nobody optimizes — battery wedge? plate thickness? — where the last
  free lunch is hiding? (Real-estate agents skim where clients don't
  look.)
- Small incentives, big behavior: a 3% mass saving compounds across
  every scenario, every kilometer. Hunt systematically for many tiny
  savings rather than one heroic feature.
