# Zen — the usefulness of what is not there

"Thirty spokes share one hub; it is the hole in the center that makes
the wheel useful." Design from emptiness.

- Ma (間), the interval: the frame is mostly air. Treat the gaps — the
  waist of an arm, the space between deck plates, the opening the wind
  passes through — as the design, and the plastic as merely its frame.
  Where can more nothing be added?
- Wabi-sabi: accept the humble material. Not carbon's perfection but
  PETG's plainness — then ask what shape makes the plain material
  sufficient. Sufficiency, not excellence, is the target: the score is
  energy per kilometer, and a frame that is exactly strong enough is
  more virtuous than one that is proudly overbuilt.
- The reed bends in the storm; the oak breaks. The storm scenario
  dominates the worst case. Consider the compliant frame — a flexible
  filament, generous width for strength but thin plates for give —
  that sways with the gust instead of fighting it.
- The middle way: after the extremes have been tried, there is a
  version of every gene that is unremarkable in all directions. Propose
  the design with no notable feature whatsoever, perfectly centered,
  and let it embarrass the clever ones.
- Beginner's mind: propose one design as if you had never seen the
  elites — only the mission. Two kilometers north, two kilometers
  south, wind. What is the least frame that does this?
